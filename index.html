Neuroma

Neuroma formation occurs because of nerve injury followed by improper intrinsic nerve repair. It is especially common in amputees where no distal target exists for the regenerating nerve end. In some cases the neuroma causes pain, and is then referred to as symptomatic neuroma.

Neuromas are benign tumors of the nervous system, and often arise post-trauma, including amputation and elective surgery. The neuroma develops as an injured nerve starts to regenerate in an uncontrolled way, resulting in a lump or unorganized axon fibers and non-neural tissue growth. The exact mechanism is unknown, yet neuroma is understood to result from misdirected Schwann cell processes causing pathophysiologic axonal outgrowth.

Morphology of nerve at various stages of neuroma development:

1. Normal nerve
2. Degenerating axons
3. Axonal sprouts
4. Unorganized bundles of axons
5. Unorganized axon growth into muscle tissue
6. Unorganized growth into fibrotic tissue (neuroma)

Current Treatment

Diagnosis is mainly clinical, and treatment is most often surgical excision. Yet, surgical excision alone does not address the potential of neuroma to re-form.

Recently, Axogen, Inc. received FDA approval for a porcine small intestine submucosa-based nerve cap to prevent/reduce neuroma. For example, in amputees; following surgical resection of the current neuroma, the nerve stump would be capped to isolate the regenerating cells and attempt to “exhaust” their growth.
