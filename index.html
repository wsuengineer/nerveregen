Introduction

Symptomatic neuroma is a painful sequela of traumatic or oncologic nerve amputations. Our goal is to reduce/prevent neuroma by isolating transected nerve stumps within protective "caps" during surgery.

Neuromas are benign tumors of the peripheral nervous system and can manifest following nerve injury, but especially amputation. A neuroma forms as an injured nerve end starts to regenerate in a disorganized way, resulting in a lump of entangled axon fibers and non-neural tissue growth. Neuroma is understood to occur from misdirected Schwann cell processes in the absence of a distal target â€” causing pathophysiologic axon outgrowth as the nerve cells try to reinstate continuity.

Aside from affecting quality of life, symptomatic neuroma can make molding a well-fitting prosthesis socket difficult, and/or prevent its successful use.

Neuroma formation phases:

1. Injured nerve end/degenerating axons
2. Axonal sprouts
3. Unorganized bundles of axons
4. Growth into muscle tissue
5. Growth into fibrous tissue (neuroma)

Current treatment for painful neuroma includes prescription drugs (does not address undelying issue), and most commonly surgical removal. The standard or care for surgical management of stump neuroma is traction neurectomy. During this technique, a neurolysis is performed and the nerve is exposed. Then the nerve is put under traction and transected to healthy nerve. Yet, traction neurectomy alone cannot prevent neuroma from re-forming as the nerve end regenerates.

User Needs and Design Inputs

User need statement: Design a biocompatible implant device to reduce neuroma formation in persons with peripheral nerve injury or neuroma.

Biocompatabilty: Device needs to produce the appropriate host response for the given body contact and duration conditions: permanent contact: implant device.

Cytotoxicity: Device material(s) cannot cause significant cell damage or death as measured by a cytotoxicity assay (the FDA requires implant devices to undergo cytotoxicity testing at 37℃ for 72 hr.).

Different sizes: Device sizes of 3 mm and 5 mm diameter to fit the range of nerve diameters for the wrist/dorsum base of the hand.

Durable: Cannot mechanically fail (e.g., tear) during and after implantation.

Ease of implementation in the clinic: Storage life of at least 32 months. Does not require custom or specific surgical instruments for implantation.

Low-cost: Cost per unit less than 20,000 USD.

Reduce pain/neuroma: Eliminate axonal outgrowth into muscle or fibrous tissue. Reduce pain (100 mm visual analog scale reading of 0 to 4 mm [no pain] or 5 to 44 mm [mild pain]).

Sterilization: Device must meet the appropriate Sterility Assurance Level.

--

Immediately after traction neurectomy, our device is implanted to cap the nerve end to isolate subsequent regeneration from surrounding tissue to reduce/prevent neuroma. We are focusing on neuroma of the wrist and dorsum base of the hand, as this region is where neuroma is most often observed in the upper limbs.

Design Concept





Design deatils:

- Collagen material. Integrates with surrounding tissue to provide a protective layer. Prevents adhesions to the surrounding soft tissue bed.
- Parabolic design to decrease device's occupied volume.
- Total length of 10 mm; 5 mm of "runway" to exhaust nerve growth (the runway length is based on several studies that showed minimal axon infiltration past 5 mm in hollow tubes).
- Partition (4 mm in length) divides regeneraring axons in two; limits entanglement.
- Cap thickness is at least 0.25 mm (based on average nerve guide conduit thickness).
- Cap is fixed to the nerve stump via photochemical tissue bonding (PTB) technique. PTB has been used effectively in other nerve repair models, and is advantageous in being atraumatic compared to medical suture.
